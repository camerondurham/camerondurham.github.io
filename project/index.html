Introduction

558 words

This exhibition contrasts how cultural objects with difficult or controversial provenance can be exhibited. We come to trust what museums present as real and verified but there are times when the history of items is unknown yet still exhibited. This exhibition contrasts how museums have exhibited work with alternative, more transparent methods used by artists to show the work in context.

The institutional power of museums establishes a level of trust in most museum visitors. When museums show work that has unknown history and authenticity, this can lead to a misunderstanding or revision of history. We trust that museums have and can use scientific research to trace the past of their collection with certainty. In reality, some items are more difficult to place or to verify. Yet, museums can still display such work with plaques citing the information they have in their collection. With the institutional power of the museum behind the work, the museumgoer usually has no choice but to accept what was presented to them. There have been many famous instances of forgery that museums failed to detect when analyzing the objects. The "Etruscan" statue that was on display at the Metropolitan Museum for decades before being proved a fake [1]. Forged cultural artifacts were accepted into the Mexican Museum's Pre-Colombian collection. These forgeries accounted for approximately 96% of this collection [2]. In these examples, the lack of information or lack of verification from the museum skewed the history of this period.

Artists Gala Porras-Kim chose to accentuate what was not known about such cultural artifacts as a form of institutional critique. For her work in the Hammer Museum's Made In LA, she focused on objects without a catalog number from the Fowler Museum. These objects lacked the generally understood indicators of provenance and history: date, medium, or site of origin. Porras-Kim reconstructed a version of what the objects could have been based on the available information. [3]. Curators form and conservators would stamp their ideas onto a work when there really was no way of knowing, due to some objects’ poor preservation. The inability to verify an object or work’s origin is brought to attention. Her work points out the role of the curator when exhibiting unverifiable artifacts. When our science cannot identify such specifics as an item’s origin and cultural background, Porras-Kim leaves its interpretation to the viewer.

In mid-2015, the ISIL fighters looted and destroyed historical artifacts in Iraq, Syria, and Libya, notably the Mosul Museum (the second largest in Iraq). [5]. The work was either lost for good or in a black art market. [4]. This work now becomes difficult to exhibit in a museum setting, given the background and possible unsavory past. Artist Morehshin Allahyari aimed to preserve these artifacts in her 3D scans and 3D prints of selected pieces destroyed by ISIS fighters in the Middle East. Each reproduced sculpture contains a storage device holding documentation of the work's destruction and correspondence with scholars regarding the work. The sculptures are made of a semi-transparent plastic material required for 3D printing. The plastic is derivative of crude oil, evoking the geopolitical and economic motivations for the war that eventually led to the objects' destruction. The material makes apparent to the viewer that the print is not an accurate or realistic reproduction of the object but instead helps capture the new history of these cultural artifacts.
